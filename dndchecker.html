DnD Character Creation Checklist

Step One - Class, Race and Name

Choose Your Character's Name

Skip this step if desired, since it’s simple.

Pick a Class

In the story, your character’s class is their profession. Game mechanic wise, it determines how your character can be played, granting you access to specific skills, proficiencies and talents.

There are many classes to choose from, with this list of classes by DnD Beyond a good place to start.

Pick a Race

In the story, your character’s race is what species they are. Similar to class, this determines traits and skills your character naturally has.

Just like classes, there are many races to pick; DnD Beyond has a list of races you can pick from.

Pick a Background

In the story, the background gives a general picture of your character’s origin story. As a mechanic, it gives your character access to additional skills and proficiencies. A character doesn’t get more traits from their background, while they would from their class.

Again, DnD Beyond provides a list of backgrounds to choose from.

Alignment

In DnD, alignments are moral guidelines for your character’s actions. They tell you how your character behaves.

There are nine total alignments in DnD:

Lawful Good Neutral Good Chaotic Good
Lawful Neutral Neutral Neutral (True Neutral) Chaotic Neutral
Lawful Evil Neutral Evil Chaotic Evil

These are guidelines and not hard rules to obey. If they feel too restrictive, feel free to not use them.

Experience Points

Some DnD campaigns are played with experience points, earned through various feats in the game. Here is where you’d record them. If you’re starting out with a level one character, write zero.

Ability Scores, Skills, and Usable Stats

Ability Scores

These determine a character’s aptitude for certain physical and mental tasks. There are six in total:

  • Strength
  • Dexterity
  • Constitution
  • Intelligence
  • Wisdom
  • Charisma

To calculate an ability score, take four d6 dice, roll them, and add together the three highest numbers. There is also a predetermined set of ability scores provided by WoTC that you can distribute among the scores, if you’d like.

Then, look at your race: this will increase some of your ability scores. For example, a Dragonborn race increases both a character’s charisma and strength by 1.

The little circle below is where you put the ability score’s modifier. This is a number you either add or subtract from dice rolls depending on what task you’re trying to accomplish. Depending on what your ability score is, your modifier will vary accordingly:

Inspiration

Inspiration points are rewarded to the player by the game master for playing one’s character true to their personality. Characters start out with no inspiration points, so leave this blank.

Proficiency Bonus

This is a number that is added to dice rolls for tasks that a character is proficient in, or good at. Proficiencies will be covered later.

This number is determined by the level of your character, and increases as you level up. At level 1, your proficiency bonus is +2.

Saving Throws

These are rolls made when your character needs to resist something, such as an attack or a spell. When the situation arises, you roll your dice and add your saving throw modifier to it.

The modifier is determined by your ability score’s modifier. Each class has proficiency in at least two ability scores, meaning you can add your proficiency bonus to the ones you’re proficient in. Find the related modifier – calculate if you’re proficient – and write it in the boxes.

Skills

These are the specific areas your character is good at. Each skill is tied to an ability score. Your character’s background and class tell you which skills you’re proficient in, meaning you can add your proficiency bonus to the modifier.

Find the related modifier – calculate if you’re proficient – and write it in the blank spaces.

Passive Perception

unlike the perception skill, passive perception relates to whether your character can notice things without deliberately paying attention.

It is calculated by adding your wisdom modifier to 10. If your character is proficient in the perception skill, add the proficiency bonus too.

Section 3

The middlemost box at the top contains your character’s armour class, initiative, speed, hit points, hit dice, and death saves.

Armour Class

the armour class is the score enemies must beat in order to deal damage to your character. For example, if your character’s armour class is 17, then an enemy attacking you must make an attack roll that’s 17 or higher. (?)

Specific classes will offer different armour class upgrades at certain levels. For example, a barbarian gets Unarmoured Defense at level 3, to which their AC can be calculated by 10 + their dexterity modifier + their constitution modifier.

If your characer is unarmoured, your armour class – AC – is 10 plus your dexterity modifier.

If your character is armoured, depending on what armour they’re wearing, you will use that armour’s AC plus your dexterity modifier. If your character is proficient in the armour they wear, they get to add their proficiency bonus to their AC. If your character isn’t, however, you will have disadvantage on ability checks, attack rolls involving dexterity or strength, saving throws, and will be unable to cast spells.

Initiative

This is a modifier you add to the dice roll that determines when you go in battle. It is equal to your dexterity modifier.

Speed

this determines how much your character can move in a round of combat. This is race-specific, but can be improved by other factors such as class. Check your race to see your character’s speed.

Hit Points

these determine your character’s health. At level 1, it can be calculated by adding your constitution modifier to a certain number. The dice you use to calculate how much HP you get in later levels are determined by your class.

Temporary hit points are hit points that don’t last forever, and can be conferred for a time by certain effects.

Hit Dice

the dice you use to calculate how much health is regained from healing. It increases with level. At level one, you get 1 hit dice, at level two, you get two, so on and so forth.

Death Saves

when your character has been knocked unconscious at 0 HP, each time your turn comes around you need to roll a d20 and add any modifiers or bonuses to it. You must beat a 10 for a success, or you get a fail. Three fails results in permanent death, while three successes means your character becomes stable at 0 HP again. This is only really relevant in battle or dangerous situations, so leave this blank.

Section 4

The last boxes contain information on attacks and spellcasting, other proficiencies and languages, equipment, and features and traits.

Attacks and Spellcasting

here is where you’d list the attacks you could make, which is reliant on what weapons your character has.

Other Proficiencies and Languages

This is where other proficiencies listed by your class can go. As well, each race and some classes allow your character rto speak certain languages.

Equipment

all characters start out with default equipment as dependent on their class. This can also be used to list certain items you pick up along your character’s journey.

Features and Traits

any abilities granted by your race and class go here.
